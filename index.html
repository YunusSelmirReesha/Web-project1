Formation of Stars

Stars are born in massive clouds of dust and gas called nebulae. Gravity pulls the gas and dust together, creating a protostar that heats up as it contracts. When the temperature and pressure become high enough, nuclear fusion ignites at the core, and a new star is born. This process can take millions of years and occurs in stellar nurseries located in galaxies.

Once a star is formed, it enters the main sequence phase where it spends most of its life fusing hydrogen into helium. The size and mass of a star determine its lifespan and the way it will eventually die—either quietly fading away or dramatically exploding as a supernova.

Understanding star formation helps scientists trace the history of the universe, study galaxy formation, and even understand the origins of elements that make up planets and life. It’s one of the most awe-inspiring phenomena in astrophysics.
